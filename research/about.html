Idle Sails · about & methods

How this chart is made

Idle Sails is a spectator’s idler: leave the tab open and a procedurally generated, historically grounded age of sail plays itself out. This page documents what stands behind the spectacle — where the numbers come from, how they were interpreted, what the simulation invents, and where it knowingly departs from the record.

Two goals, held together

Every design decision answers to two co-equal aims. The first is simulative: a zero-friction, deterministic world — the same seed always produces the same sea, at any clock speed, which is what makes closing the tab and returning days later exact rather than approximate. The whole control surface is a speed slider and click-to-inspect; there is nothing to build, buy, or win.

The second is historical sensitization. The dataset is built knowing that the archive is not the past (after Michel-Rolph Trouillot’s Silencing the Past): the surviving quantitative record of this era is Euro-centric, institutional, and full of silences that enter at every stage — at the making of sources, of archives, and of narratives. The chart tries to be honest about that rather than hiding it behind a confident-looking map. The rules it follows are set out in the project charter: no silent zeros (a trade known to have existed is never implicitly absent), no fabricated precision (rank only where sources support ranking), basin-local assembly (no forced commensuration between seas with incommensurable records), declared boundaries (every dataset states what it excludes and why), a public silences register, and sober treatment of coerced human movement wherever it appears.

How the world sails itself

The simulation is a seeded, deterministic world running a flowing clock from 1550 to 1815, after which the chart is “redrawn” over a five-year ramp and the cycle begins again. Every vessel is generated the moment she puts to sea: her lane is drawn from the era’s trade-flow weights, then her type, flag, tonnage, cargo, name, and master follow from period-specific vocabularies, and her entire fate — every leg, every port call, whether and when she is lost — is rolled at spawn from her own random stream. Nothing about her is decided later, which is why fast-forwarding a saved world in a few big steps lands on exactly the sea that leaving the tab open would have produced.

Ships move along pre-computed route polylines: curved, seasonal tracks baked offline by a least-time router over parametric wind and current fields with per-class sailing polars (square-riggers cannot point closer than ≈60° to the wind; the monsoon reverses; the Roaring Forties carry the eastbound Indiamen). Junks and dhows sail their own polars. Passage durations were calibrated against recorded at-sea sailing times — Middle Passage legs within a fraction of a percent of the SlaveVoyages medians, England to the East Indies against East India Company records — with the known residuals documented in the project’s calibration report rather than smoothed away.

Where the traffic comes from

Since 2026 the sim spawns from an evidence-classed trade-system flow matrix: sixty-odd named trade systems (the Carrera de Indias, the Sound-toll Baltic trades, the VOC spice circuit, the Nanyang junk trade, the English coastal colliers…), each carrying per-decade voyage-count ranges assembled basin by basin from the sources that basin actually has. Each seeded world draws one reading from every range — two seeds may honestly disagree about how busy a trade was, because the historians do. Port prominence is an output of these flows, not an input: the 1560s’ busiest port on this chart is Istanbul, a city the ranked European record could never place there. See prominence, derived from the flows.

The anchor series behind the counted systems include the Sound Toll Registers (Baltic passages, 1497–1857), the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database (SlaveVoyages), Chaunu’s reconstruction of the Carrera de Indias from the Seville records, Dutch-Asiatic Shipping (the VOC’s complete voyage list), the Nagasaki registers kept under the Tokugawa trade restrictions (where a state did count, Chinese hulls outnumber Dutch ten to one — the anchor for the junk-trade reconstruction), the Canton trade records of the European companies, and the Marseille échelles du Levant series. Where no series exists, systems are classed as reconstructions from scholarship or as declared assertions with wide bounds — never as silent zeros.

The evidence classes

Click any vessel and her ledger ends with one italic line saying what kind of evidence stands behind the trade her voyage samples. There are five:

counted
A surviving series records this trade — a toll register, a customs house, a company ledger. Even these are soft ground: the Sound registers are biased by evasion and exemption, and the register says so.
proxied
Inferred from adjacent records — a trade visible only in someone else’s books.
reconstructed
Scholarship’s estimate, not a surviving ledger — strong secondary literature (the Nanyang junk trade after Blussé, Reid, and Sugihara).
asserted
Our own stated estimate where the archive is silent — wide bounds, reasoning on record (Caribbean smuggling, the Chinese coastal grain fleets). An assertion you can see is safer than a zero you can’t.
state
A naval voyage — outside the commercial record entirely.

How the ports were chosen

The world sails 66 ports, chosen in three layers. The backbone is the flow matrix itself: the ports the sixty systems need (Hamburg, Danzig, Riga, St Petersburg, Venice, Genoa, Marseille, Boston, New York, Havana, Surat, Calcutta, Amoy…). On top of that sits a curated diversity tranche — minor ports that never troubled a ranking but carry a monopoly, a chokepoint, a single precious cargo, or the flag of an otherwise excluded people (Dejima, Tranquebar, Banda Neira, Naha). Last come six declared exceptions kept for what they say about the record itself — Istanbul above all, present without a rank for decades because the sources support presence but not ranking; that state is data, not a defect.

Ports also live and die: founding and destruction windows are enforced against every lane (no Kingston before 1655; Smeerenburg’s blubber town burns out by 1660; Kaffa closes in 1783), a destroyed harbour keeps a faint ruin mark because the chart remembers, and the dot speaks the dominant port name of its time — Jayakarta before Batavia, Port Royal before Kingston, Hugli before Calcutta, St John’s for the Banks fishery outside Louisbourg’s brief French decades.

Reading the chart

Ship glyphs — shape is class, colour is flag

  • Slim dart — merchantman, brig, snow, sloop: the ordinary carriers of the sea.
  • Full rounded hull — East Indiaman, fluyt, and the great hulls of the earlier era: galleon, carrack, junk.
  • Sharp barbed arrowhead — frigate, sloop-of-war.
  • Broad hexagonal hull — ship of the line.
  • Boxy hull with a transverse bar — slave ship. The bar is deliberate and sober: these hulls should never read as just another trader.

Colour always means allegiance — the legend lists every flag. Company ships (EIC, VOC, WIC, the Danish and Swedish East India companies…) fly their company’s colour and take their naming culture from their parent nation.

Masters and captains

Every vessel sails under a named shipmaster, drawn from period naming pools per culture. The title travels where the history put it: European naval commanders are captains and merchant shipmasters are masters, while in the Indian Ocean the nakhoda — the shipowner-master of the dhow and Mughal marine — carries the title in the name, Ottoman masters are … Reis (with Greek kapetans alongside, as that empire’s marine truly sailed), and Makassar’s masters carry Daeng. These are plausible names, not attested individuals; any resemblance to a real voyage is the period’s, not the record’s.

The sober treatments

The Middle Passage and other coerced flows. The trans-Atlantic slave trade is historically central to this era’s shipping and is therefore in the data — but it is never gamified. Slave ships carry no cargo value, no profit framing, and no reward of any kind; their ledgers state factually that the vessel carries enslaved people held captive aboard, and a loss records that they were lost with the ship. The same charter rule extends to every promoted coerced flow — the Black Sea trade at Kaffa and the Indian Ocean slave trades sail under validator-enforced sober framing of their own.

Where the chart knowingly differs from the record

  • Vessels are statistical samples, not registered hulls. Each ship stands for the traffic of her lane in her decade; names, masters, and tonnages are plausible vocabulary, not archival entries.
  • One seed, one reading. Each world realizes a single value from every trade’s [low, high] evidence range. Uncertainty lives between worlds, not within one.
  • Coverage is partial and declared. The folded flow systems account for roughly 76–89% of recorded world voyage volume depending on the decade; the remainder is either unsailable at this port roster or lives in the silences register.
  • Spawn pacing is clamped. Traffic follows the realized totals but is held within 0.5–1.6× the era mean, so the 1550s never feel empty and the 1810s never swamp the renderer — a spectator concession, on record.
  • A residual floor keeps authored lanes alive. About 3% of volume is spread across era-active lanes so no authored trade fully vanishes between decades; naval movements are a fixed ~6% pool outside the commercial matrix.
  • Weather is climatology, not events. Routes are seasonal averages; the one era-climate signal is a bounded +7% passage-time factor on North Atlantic legs during the Maunder Minimum (1645–1715). Hurricane and wartime risk raise loss odds, not individual storms.
  • The loop is a fiction. History does not restart in 1550; the five-year “redrawing” ramp exists so an idler can run forever, and the era label says so while it happens.
