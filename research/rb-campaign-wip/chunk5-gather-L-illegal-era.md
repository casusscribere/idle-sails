# Chunk 5 gather L — illegal-era slave trade + suppression squadron (raw, pre-refutation)
# SOBER REGISTER: factual language only; no value tier, no profit framing, never a reward.

[L-01] 2M+ enslaved Africans imported into Brazil 1800–1850 (Bethell; Brazil all-time ~4.9M disembarked). reconstructed/high.
[L-02] 750,000+ imported into Brazil during the 1830s–40s, when the trade was entirely illegal under Brazilian law. reconstructed/high.
[L-03] 371,615 arrived in Brazil 1840–1851 (British FO returns via Bethell p.70) — a FLOOR (concealed landings escaped the count). counted/high.
[L-04] ~128,000 into Brazil 1831–39 (FO memo 1844) — pre-1840 data imprecise; modern reconstructions run ~200–300k. counted/LOW — lower bound only.
[L-05] Annual surge figures: 50,324 (1846), ~60,000 (1848); 1847 commonly ~56,000 (unverified this pass). counted/medium.
[L-06] ~150,000 arrived 1847–49 — the illegal-era peak, partly anticipation of British suppression post-Aberdeen. reconstructed/medium.
[L-07] After the Eusébio de Queirós Law (4 Sept 1850): −80%+ by end-1851 (~3,000 in 1851), effectively extinct 1852 (residual to ~1856). reconstructed/high.
[L-08] 1815 Anglo-Portuguese treaty banned slaving north of the equator; 1817 Additional Convention created the first Courts of Mixed Commission; SOUTH of the equator (Angola→Brazil) legal for Portuguese/Brazilian subjects until 1830. counted/high.
[L-09] 1826 treaty made the trade illegal for Brazilians from 1830; the 7 Nov 1831 Feijó law declared subsequent arrivals legally free but went unenforced ("para inglês ver"); genuine trough 1831–35. counted/high.
[L-10] Aberdeen Act (8 Aug 1845): British courts tried Brazilian slavers (19 of 33 St Helena cases Brazilian, H1 1848); volume nonetheless ROSE late-1840s. counted/high.
[L-11] Mozambique exports ~15,000/yr in the 1820s–30s; 400,000+ carried Mozambique→Brazil 1800–1865 — the route GREW because of patrol pressure on the west coast. reconstructed/medium.
[L-12] Rio region ~2.3M disembarked over the whole trade — the largest single disembarkation region in the Americas. reconstructed/high.
[L-13] Anglo-Spanish 1817 treaty (£400,000 payment): north-of-equator ban immediate, entire from 1820; 1835 treaty added the equipment clause (exact date reconstructed); Spain's 1845 penal law; Cuban trade continued to 1867. counted/high.
[L-14] Registered: 372,449 to Cuba before the legal end; "at least" 123,775 1821–53 — explicit undercount, reconstructions 2–3× higher. counted/medium.
[L-15] Cuban decade reconstructions: ~79,900 (1821–30), ~112,500 (1831–40), ~126,100 (1841–50) — but other series (Eltis 1987; Murray) put the 1840s at ~50–60k. GENUINE disagreement; wide bounds, no point value. reconstructed/LOW.
[L-16] Cuba total ~780–800k; longest illegal participation, to 1867 (final voyage). reconstructed/high.
[L-17] After 1850 almost all Cuba-serving slavers (~500 vessels) American-built/flagged, many New York connections; US flag defeated British search until Lyons–Seward (1862). reconstructed/medium.
[L-18] Havana Mixed Commission adjudicated cases covering 10,391 people 1824–41 — a small fraction of the traffic. counted/medium.
[L-19] West Africa Squadron established 1808 with two vessels (Solebay, Derwent). counted/high.
[L-20] Strength: 2 (1808) → ~6 (Collier 1818, ~3,000 mi of coast) → ~25 vessels/~2,000 personnel + ~1,000 Kru sailors by mid-century (one source ~11 by 1811; Hutt committee nearly abolished it c.1848–50; many later vessels paddle steamers). counted/high.
[L-21] 1808–60: ~1,600 slave ships captured, ~150,000 people freed (~30 captures/yr, concentrated after 1835). counted/high.
[L-22] Interception ~6–10% of slaving voyages; effectiveness genuinely debated (deterrence/cost-raising vs under-resourced); far higher in specific theatres (Brazilian waters 1850–51). reconstructed/medium.
[L-23] Mortality figures IRRECONCILABLE as stated (~1,600 deaths 1830–65 vs ~17,000 whole-campaign): frame only as "among the navy's deadliest stations" — never a point value. reconstructed/LOW.
[L-24] Infrastructure: Freetown naval station 1819, Ascension supply, Cape Town 1832. counted/high.
[L-25] Freetown Vice-Admiralty Court 1807 → Mixed Commissions 1817–19 (six courts by 1820); 600+ ships condemned, ~80,000 released; ~99,752 liberated Africans landed at Freetown 1808–63. counted/high.
[L-26] Anglo-Spanish court at Sierra Leone: 251 vessels condemned, 25,000+ people, 1819–45. counted/medium.
[L-27] Equipment-clause gap closed in the 1830s; Palmerston Act 1839 (Portuguese vessels), Aberdeen Act 1845 (Brazilian) — drove flag-switching to the US ensign. counted/high.
[L-28] 1850–51: RN entered Brazilian waters/harbours (fire exchanged with a fort once); trade resolved within ~12 months — historiography credits the CONJUNCTION with Brazil's own law. counted/high.
[L-29] US Africa Squadron (1843–61, Webster–Ashburton 80-gun clause): 4 ships/88 guns initially; based Cape Verde (~2,500 nmi from the Bights) until Luanda 1859; 19 crews tried in 16 years; ~100 captures 1820–61 all squadrons, most after 1859. Generally judged ineffective pre-1859. counted/high.
[L-30] HMS Black Joke (ex-slaver Henriqueta): 11 slavers in one year — the tactical lesson: only fast ex-slavers/steamers caught the clippers. counted/high.
[L-31] Illegal-era vessels: Baltimore-clipper schooners >12 kn; Henriqueta landed 3,000+ over ~6 voyages pre-capture; late 1840s Brazil also used larger vessels/steamers (per-voyage 200–1,000+). reconstructed/high.
[L-32] ~a quarter of ALL transatlantic transportation occurred after 1807 — carry as [2.5M, 3.2M] embarked in the suppression era (a 1.8M outlier uses a narrower definition). reconstructed/medium.
[L-33] DERIVED Brazil voyage unit: 371,615 (1840–51) at 250–600/voyage ⇒ ~700–1,200 voyages ≈ 60–110 arrivals/yr, 1846–49 peak ~90–150/yr. asserted/medium — explicit derivation.
[L-34] DERIVED Cuba voyage unit: ~20–50/yr (1820s), ~30–60 (1830s), ~15–45 (1840s, decade totals disagreeing 2×). asserted/LOW — wide bounds are the honest state.
[L-35] Principal routes: Angola/Congo North (Cabinda, Ambriz, Benguela, Luanda) + post-1830 Mozambique/Quelimane → Rio/Santos; Bight of Benin (Ouidah/Lagos) → Bahia; Upper Guinea (Gallinas) + Bights → Havana/Matanzas. South-of-equator legality to 1830 explains the Brazil lanes' persistence. reconstructed/high.
